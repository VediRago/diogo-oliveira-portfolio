# Chapter Four: What Came From the Sky

I was there when they brought him in.

The Servans were the first to see it. They claimed they were tending the east fields near the lake when the light came. So bright it turned day into night.

I didn't fully understand what I was witnessing. The Servans brought a child wrapped in cloth, and my father got angry. I remember him asking if they were mocking him.

The child could not have been more than eleven or twelve. He was naked, dirty. But his eyes—they were black. Black all the way through. His posture matched their claim. If he fell from the sky, he would feel distant. And he was.

We stood there for a long time. I didn't see him blink once. He just stared at the ground, as if he couldn't hear anything.

The only fear in that hall came from the Servans. I couldn't understand why. Why would someone fear bringing a child who fell from the sky?

My father was turning his back on the child when he raised his voice.

"You expect me to believe my house has been visited by a god, and the god arrived through my servants' quarters instead of my front gate."

"We don't ask you to believe anything," Isildos said. "We ask you to look at him."

My father was not amused by being told what to do inside his own hall. He called for the guard captain and gave the order. I'd seen him do this many times. But the outcome would be different.

The guard went toward them. The intention was to punish. I didn't see the child move. But somehow, the guard was thrown across the hall.

He did not scream. He did not run. He simply put himself between the guard and Isildos. Something in the boy's small body did something none of us had words for. The guard left his feet entirely. He crossed half the receiving hall through the air and met the wall hard enough that the sound silenced the room.

My father looked at the boy for a long moment. Calculating, I think. What a boy who could do that might be worth to a House that had spent generations being merely influential.

"Take him to the east wing," Father said. His voice had changed entirely. "Feed him. Clothe him. No one touches him again."

"And no one outside this hall speaks of what just happened here."

That night, the three Servans were summoned.

I was not present. I only know what I learned much later: none of the three were ever seen again.

The boy asked about Isildos in the weeks after, in broken pieces of language.

"The warm one, where is the warm one?"

My father told him she had been sent to serve a different household, far away.

And I said nothing. I was too young to fully understand what I had witnessed.

So I carried it. The way I would carry many things in the years that followed.

In silence.
