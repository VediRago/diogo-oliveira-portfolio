# Case Study 01 — Building a World to Test Quest Design

← [Back to README](../README.md)

## Main Problem

Creating quests from nothing did not feel immersive.

I could invent quest ideas, but that did not prove the quests belonged to a world, its people, or its history.

I wanted quests to originate from the environment around them, from the pressure already established.

## Main Solution

Instead of creating quests directly, I built the conditions that could generate them.

The goal was to create a world with enough history that quest opportunities could appear as consequences rather than isolated tasks.

## Problem 01 — Time

The Erit worldbuilding project showed me several problems right away.

The first one seemed to be the name of the world. But I quickly realized that was not the most important problem to solve.

The real problem was something I had not thought about at first: time.

I learned that time is what anchors a civilization. It creates points in the past that generate pressure around the world the player steps into.

Even when the player does not know every historical detail, those points help maintain plausibility.

## Solution

I created a calendar to establish a shared reference point between the setting’s past and present.

## Problem 02 — Calendar Origin

Creating a calendar solved the problem of time, but it immediately created another question.

Why did the calendar begin there?

A calendar without an origin felt arbitrary. If it was going to anchor the history of a civilization, the starting point needed to matter.

It needed to represent something important enough that people would continue measuring time from it generations later.

## Solution

I decided the calendar would begin from a statue already mentioned in NOEME.

That small reference gave the calendar an anchor that was not invented separately from the project.

The statue then needed a history.

That history led to the creation of Viriatus.

## Problem 03 — The Figure Behind the Statue

The statue already carried importance because it was connected to a character in NOEME.

When I used it as the calendar origin, the real problem became the person behind the statue.

If the calendar began from that point, the figure attached to the statue needed to be important enough to anchor a civilization's memory.

## Solution

I created Viriatus.

The statue became a way to give that figure weight, similar to how monuments or religious icons can preserve meaning across generations.

## Problem 04 — Public Meaning

Creating Viriatus gave the calendar a figure, but his importance still needed a consequence.

He was killed, and his name, achievements, and death were used by the Ventari family to create Regulatus.

## Solution

Viriatus became the first stone used to build the Empire under Ventari manipulation.

## Problem 05 — Turning Viriatus Into Proof

Viriatus could not simply be remembered as a powerful figure.

If House Ventari wanted to control what came after him, they needed his life to prove something about them.

They could not publicly say they found him.

They needed the public to believe they formed him.

So he became the myth of Regulatus, a symbol of what House Ventari claimed its discipline could create.

## Solution

Viriatus became proof of Ventari discipline.

His victories were used to argue that the old order failed where Ventari teaching, hierarchy, and control succeeded.

## Remaining Chain To Finish

Regulatus still needs to connect back to the original design problem: quests.

The remaining sections should explain how Regulatus becomes inherited pressure, how that pressure shapes people and factions, and how those conditions create quest opportunities.
