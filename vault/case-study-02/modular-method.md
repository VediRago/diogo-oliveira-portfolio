# Modular Method

This support page defines the modular narrative method used in Case Study 02.

The method begins with a simple chain:

```text
Experience → Emotion → Reason → Behavior
```

This chain is used when the goal is to understand a character or group response. A person does not act because the writer needs an action. They act because something happened to them, that experience created emotion, the emotion formed a reason, and the reason produced behavior.

When the subject becomes a place, culture, faction, or city, the chain can extend:

```text
Experience → Emotion → Reason → Behavior → Atmosphere
```

Atmosphere is not added separately. It emerges from repeated behavior. A place feels orderly, tense, watched, proud, afraid, or ceremonial because people inside it have learned to act that way.

When that atmosphere survives long enough to become law, ritual, record, doctrine, inherited belief, or institution, the chain can extend again:

```text
Experience → Emotion → Reason → Behavior → Atmosphere → History
```

This prevents worldbuilding from becoming decorative. History is only added when the earlier layers have created something durable enough to be inherited.

For quests and gameplay, the method extends only when there is enough pressure to create a playable situation:

```text
Experience → Emotion → Reason → Behavior → Atmosphere → History → Quest Logic
```

The rule is simple: do not extend the chain unless the next layer is earned.

A character may only need the base chain. A city may need atmosphere. A doctrine may need history. A quest may need the full chain because the player needs pressure, obstacles, possible approaches, and consequences.

The method stays the same. The depth changes.
