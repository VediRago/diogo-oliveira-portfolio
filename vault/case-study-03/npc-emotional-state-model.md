# NPC Emotional State Model

## Purpose

This file records a design principle for creating believable NPCs in Erit's world.

The goal is to avoid NPCs feeling like cardboard boxes carrying general dialogue and generic reactions.

NPCs should not only respond to player actions as universal game inputs.

They should respond through emotional history, location pressure, group identity, and personal background.

## Core Principle

NPC behavior should come from emotional history, not universal reaction rules.

A character has a reason to behave a certain way because of their emotional state.

That state comes from what they lived through.

It can also be modified by later experiences, including the player's actions.

The basic model is:

**past experience → current emotional state → likely reaction → modified by new experience**

## Emotional Gravity

Characters easily fall into certain emotions because life trained them to expect certain outcomes.

This can be seen in the four founding siblings:

- Rutare was sheltered and pressured, so he easily falls into inadequacy, avoidance, and quiet control.
- Ilunis was used and maimed, so he easily falls into anger, jealousy, entitlement, and punishment.
- Teresta was dismissed and cast out, so she easily falls into distance, obsession, proving value, and control through knowledge.
- Ermerus was given freedom without discipline, so he easily falls into charm, interpretation, appetite, and control through meaning.

Smaller NPCs can work the same way.

A homeless man who has been treated like trash may fall quickly into suspicion.

A guard raised in Regulatus may fall quickly into duty and fear of shame.

A rebel betrayed before may fall quickly into distrust.

A doctor trapped by Teresta systems may fall quickly into guilt or self-preservation.

## Context Changes Meaning

The value of an action depends on the emotional state of the person receiving it.

Example:

Giving a weapon to one NPC may build trust because they feel safer.

Giving a weapon to a war-traumatized NPC may damage trust because it makes them feel unseen.

To that NPC, the weapon may mean:

- You do not see me.
- You see a soldier.
- You are asking me to become the thing that broke me again.

This is stronger than a universal rule like:

**gift item = affection up**

The same object, phrase, or choice should not mean the same thing to every character.

Meaning comes from emotional history.

## Why Some Games Feel Shallow

Many games begin to feel shallow when NPCs are not linked to emotional history or social pressure.

Once the player realizes they are surrounded by generic dialogue containers, immersion breaks.

The problem is not that every NPC needs a huge backstory.

The problem is that NPC reactions often lack layered conditions.

A city should not feel like a collection of interchangeable people.

It should feel like a place that shapes how people talk, fear, trust, lie, and survive.

## Layered NPC Conditions

NPCs should be built through layers.

### Layer 1 — Location Layer

This is specific to the city, district, clinic, alley, arena, or settlement.

It should define the pressure everyone in that location feels.

Examples:

- a city under Ilunis military presence
- an alley network outside Rutare records
- a clinic controlled by Teresta chip systems
- a district proud of Regulatus discipline
- a rebel-held ruin where trust is scarce

This layer should usually be location-specific and not reused everywhere.

If every city uses the same pressure, every city will feel the same.

### Layer 2 — Group Layer

This defines the pressure of the NPC's group, family, class, faction, or social role.

Examples:

- Regulatus loyalist
- rebel sympathizer
- unregistered person
- clinic worker
- Ilunis guard
- Rutare clerk
- Teresta doctor
- Servan-descended worker
- alley survivor

This layer should also be specific enough to the place and faction.

A rebel in one city should not feel exactly like a rebel in another if their conditions are different.

### Layer 3 — Personal NPC Layer

This is the individual layer.

It includes dialogue style, intended background, emotional wound, trust threshold, fears, habits, and personal memory.

This is the only layer that can be somewhat reused as a pattern.

For example, several NPCs can share an archetype like suspicious survivor, proud loyalist, guilty doctor, or exhausted guard.

But the location and group layers should change how that archetype behaves.

## Example Layering

A homeless man in a wealthy district should not behave exactly like a homeless man in a rebel alley.

The personal archetype may be similar: hungry, cautious, observant.

But the layers change the reaction.

### Wealthy District

Location pressure: watched by guards, treated as filth, chased away from clean streets.

Group pressure: unregistered, unwanted, used to being ignored.

Personal state: suspicious, but remembers kindness.

This NPC may help if the player showed compassion earlier, but resent being paid only when useful.

### Dangerous Alley

Location pressure: survival through traps, theft, and informal gangs.

Group pressure: people outside the system must exploit opportunity.

Personal state: charming, opportunistic, predatory.

This NPC may flatter the player and lead them into a mugging.

The same broad NPC type becomes different because the layers are different.

## Harsh Reality Rule

The world should not say:

**Poor people are good. Rich people are bad.**

It should say:

**A harsh world shapes everyone.**

Some people become kind despite suffering.

Some become cruel because of it.

Some switch depending on hunger, fear, and opportunity.

Characters are not special by default.

Most are just people trying to survive the reality they were born into.

## Design Use

This model should shape future NPCs, dialogue, and quest branching.

Before writing an NPC reaction, ask:

- What location pressure shapes this person?
- What group pressure shapes this person?
- What personal emotional state shapes this person?
- What past experience makes them likely to react this way?
- How can the player's action modify that emotional state?

This keeps NPCs from feeling generic.

It also makes quest branching more believable because the same player action can produce different outcomes depending on who receives it.
