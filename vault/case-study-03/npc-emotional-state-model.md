# NPC Emotional State Model

## Purpose

This file records a design principle for creating believable NPCs in Erit's world.

NPCs need emotional history, location pressure, group identity, and personal background. Their reactions should feel shaped by what they have lived through, where they are, and what kind of pressure their group carries.

## Core Principle

NPC behavior should come from emotional history instead of universal reaction rules.

A character has a reason to behave a certain way because of their emotional state. That state comes from what they lived through and can be changed by later experiences, including the player's actions.

The basic model is:

**past experience → current emotional state → likely reaction → modified by new experience**

## Emotional Gravity

Characters easily fall into certain emotions because life trained them to expect certain outcomes.

The four founding siblings show this pattern clearly. Rutare was sheltered and pressured, so he falls into inadequacy, avoidance, and quiet control. Ilunis was used and maimed, so he falls into anger, jealousy, entitlement, and punishment. Teresta was dismissed and cast out, so she falls into distance, obsession, proving value, and control through knowledge. Ermerus was given freedom without discipline, so he falls into charm, interpretation, appetite, and control through meaning.

Smaller NPCs can work the same way. A homeless man who has been treated like trash may fall quickly into suspicion. A guard raised in Regulatus may fall quickly into duty and fear of shame. A rebel betrayed before may fall quickly into distrust. A doctor trapped by Teresta systems may fall quickly into guilt or self-preservation.

## Context Changes Meaning

The value of an action depends on the emotional state of the person receiving it.

Giving a weapon to one NPC may build trust because they feel safer. Giving a weapon to a war-traumatized NPC may damage trust because it makes them feel unseen.

To that NPC, the weapon may say: you see a soldier, you are asking me to become the thing that broke me again, and you missed what I actually needed.

This is stronger than a universal rule like:

**gift item = affection up**

The same object, phrase, or choice should carry different meanings for different characters. Meaning comes from emotional history.

## Why Some Games Feel Shallow

Many games begin to feel shallow when NPCs lack emotional history or social pressure.

Once the player realizes they are surrounded by generic dialogue containers, immersion breaks.

Every NPC needs layered conditions more than a huge backstory. A city should feel like a place that shapes how people talk, fear, trust, lie, and survive.

## Layered NPC Conditions

NPCs should be built through layers.

### Layer 1 — Location Layer

This layer is specific to the city, district, clinic, alley, arena, or settlement. It defines the pressure everyone in that location feels.

A city under Ilunis military presence creates different reactions from an alley network outside Rutare records, a clinic controlled by Teresta chip systems, a district proud of Regulatus discipline, or a rebel-held ruin where trust is scarce.

This layer should usually be location-specific. If every city uses the same pressure, every city will feel the same.

### Layer 2 — Group Layer

This layer defines the pressure of the NPC's group, family, class, faction, or social role.

A Regulatus loyalist, rebel sympathizer, unregistered person, clinic worker, Ilunis guard, Rutare clerk, Teresta doctor, Servan-descended worker, or alley survivor all carry different forms of group pressure.

This layer should be specific enough to the place and faction. A rebel in one city should feel different from a rebel in another when their conditions are different.

### Layer 3 — Personal NPC Layer

This is the individual layer.

It includes dialogue style, intended background, emotional wound, trust threshold, fears, habits, and personal memory.

This layer can reuse patterns. Several NPCs can share an archetype like suspicious survivor, proud loyalist, guilty doctor, or exhausted guard. The location and group layers should change how that archetype behaves.

## Example Layering

A homeless man in a wealthy district should behave differently from a homeless man in a rebel alley.

The personal archetype may be similar: hungry, cautious, observant. The layers change the reaction.

### Wealthy District

The location pressure is guard surveillance, social disgust, and removal from clean streets. The group pressure is being unregistered, unwanted, and used to being ignored. The personal state is suspicious, with memory of kindness.

This NPC may help if the player showed compassion earlier, but resent being paid only when useful.

### Dangerous Alley

The location pressure is survival through traps, theft, and informal gangs. The group pressure is life outside the system, where people exploit opportunity to survive. The personal state is charming, opportunistic, and predatory.

This NPC may flatter the player and lead them into a mugging.

The same broad NPC type becomes different because the layers are different.

## Harsh Reality Rule

The world should reject simple moral sorting.

A harsh world shapes everyone.

Some people become kind despite suffering. Some become cruel because of it. Some switch depending on hunger, fear, and opportunity.

Characters are not special by default. Most are just people trying to survive the reality they were born into.

## Design Use

This model should shape future NPCs, dialogue, and quest branching.

Before writing an NPC reaction, identify the location pressure, group pressure, personal emotional state, past experience, and how the player's action can modify that emotional state.

This keeps NPCs from feeling generic.

It also makes quest branching more believable because the same player action can produce different outcomes depending on who receives it.
